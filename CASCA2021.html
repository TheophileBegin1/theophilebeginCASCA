Discovery of extended emission in the galaxy cluster MS 0735.6+7421

T. Bégin, J. Hlavacek-Larrondo, C. Rhea, B. McNamara, M. Gendron-Marsollais, R. J. van Weeren

What's crucial about the galaxy cluster MS 0735.6+7421?

Radio mini-halos

  • A mini-halo is a diffuse radio emission structure mostly found at the center of massive cool-core clusters.
  • It has a typical scale of 100-500 kpc.
  • These structures are believed to be the result of in-situ (re-)acceleration of electron populations.
  • The main hypothesis for the (re-)acceleration of electron populations is turbulence induced by the sloshing movement of the intracluster medium (ICM).
  • However, recent observational work suggests that the (re-)acceleration mechanism might be linked to the central AGN and thus, more radio observations are required in order to investigate this hypothesis.

MS 0735.6+7421

  • MS 0735.6+7421 is a massive cool-core cluster and so, it is a likely cluster candidate to host a mini-halo.
  • Moreover, it exhibits one of the most powerful central AGN known and is thus a key limiting case to investigate the hypothesis of a (re-)acceleration of electrons reponsible for the mini-halo emission by the central AGN.

MS 0735.6+7421 key features

  • Redshift: z = 0.216 (corresponding to 3.5 kpc per arcsec).
  • Mass: M500 = (4.5 ± 1.2) x 1014 M⊙.
  • Central entropy: K0 = (12.6 ± 0.6) keV cm2.
  • Mean power of the jets: Pjets = (1.7 ± 0.6) x 1046 erg/s.

Our results

Click on the header to learn more!

  • The detailed morphology of the relativistic jets were image with the Very Large Array (VLA) in P-band (230-470 MHz) A configuration and published in McNamara et al. 2009.

  • No diffuse extended radio emission was discovered as part of this McNamara et al. 2009 paper. This is not unexpected given the configuration that was used (VLA A configuration observations benefit from higher spatial resolution, but lower sensitivity to radio flux density).
  • Imaging of the central part of the cluster with higher sensitivity to flux density could allow to detect a diffuse emission (see the box titled ''MS 0735.6+7421'').

  • We produced two new JVLA radio images of MS 0735.6+7421 in L-band (1-2 GHz) C configuration (see fig. 2) and in P-band (230-470 MHz) C configuration (see fig. 3). The key advantage of these images is that they are more sensitive to the faint emission (thus allowing to detect emission that is fainter and more diffuse).

  • The L-band (1-2 GHz) image shows extended diffuse emission larger than the previously detected jets. However, it is not yet clear if this new diffuse emission is attributable to the jets or to a more extended diffuse emission. We note that the extended emission is on the order of 500 kpc which is typical for a mini-halo structures.
  • By comparing the total flux density of the old P-band (230-470 MHz) A configuration image (SAconfig,350MHz = 0.60±0.05 Jy) with the new P-band (230-470 MHz) C configuration image (SCconfig,350MHz = 0.70±0.06 Jy). By scaling these flux to 1.4 GHz and comparing the associated radio power, we find an increase in radio power of Pexcess,1.4GHz = (5±3)x1024 W/Hz, which is consistent with typical mini-halos.

  • We see a clear spectral index gradient from a value of approximately 2 in the core to a value of 4 in the outskirt.
  • The central value of 2 is very steep for a typical AGN spectral index. We conclude that this is due to the superposition of jet emission with the central core emission.
  • The outskirt value is also very steep with a value of 4 suggesting spectral ageing of the electron population responsible for the emission.

Conclusions

  • We discovered a diffuse radio emission with a radio power that is consistent with typical radio power for mini-halos.
  • The spectral index maps is typical with a ageing population of electrons.
